# Product Support Customer Tenets

Purpose
Purpose of this document
This resource will provide Product Support guidance on how to deliver an excellent
customer experience when interacting with our customers.
It will provide 5 tenets or guidelines that should be applied to each customer interaction,
as well as practical examples on how to apply those guidelines. These guidelines are
generic enough that can and should be applied to all customer types, including Property
Managers, Residents, and Service Providers.
How do Customer Service Tenets tie into our Leadership Principle of Customer
Obsession?
We arenʼt just customer-centric, we are obsessed with our customers. We consider every
user of Latchelʼs services to be a customer. We want every user to have the best possible
experience using our services and interacting with our employees. Being customer-
obsessed means creating a positive experience for our customers every time they interact
with us

Tenet 4 – Solve the Problem
We strive to fulfill our customersʼ needs and resolve their concerns effectively.
While we aim to resolve every issue, some problems may be outside our control or subject to
approvals beyond our authority.
Our goal is to:
Answer all questions and address all concerns, including any related or account issues that
arise.
Prevent new problems from occurring as we resolve the initial issue.
Ensure the customer understands and agrees with the resolution.
Help the customer leave the interaction feeling positive or at least that the resolution was
fair and reasonable.

Tenet 5 - Escalate Problems
We seek help and escalate potential or actual problems as they happen.
What escalation means
Escalation is the act of increasing the urgency, visibility, or level of intervention for a
problem.When we escalate, the problem is either out of our control or risks getting worse
without immediate support.
You should escalate when:
Youʼve done your research and still donʼt know what to do,
You donʼt have access or authority to take the next step,
The customer is demanding to speak with a supervisor or CSM.
There is an obvious sense of frustration, irritation, and/or anger.
In short, escalate when you canʼt move the issue forward, or when you believe there is a
churn risk. These are signs that the problem needs a higher level of attention—fast.
Escalation can take many forms: looping in a team lead, submitting an internal ticket, or flagging
a trend youʼve spotted. Itʼs not always about asking someone else to “take over”—itʼs often
about bringing in the right partner to help resolve the issue quickly and correctly.
