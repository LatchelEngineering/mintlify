## (1) Receive the Submission & Review the Complaint
When a Deep Dive (DD) request is received, the Product Support Specialist carefully reviews the
specific issue or inquiry to ensure a clear understanding of:
The problem being raised
The impact of the issue
Any supporting information provided by the customer
Best Practice: Always read the title and description of the ticket first to get an overview of the
deep dive youʼre about to conduct.
Visibility: Keep the Team Owner and Viewer set to PS Deep Dive Tickets to ensure
the entire Product Support team can view and collaborate as needed.
When reviewing a Deep Dive ticket, follow these steps:

## (2) Use Resources & Build the Timeline
The first step in any deep dive is to gather all relevant information surrounding the issue. This
means pulling data from every available source — including support tickets, customer
communications, system logs, data analytics, and any other resources that can help clarify what
happened. The goal is to build a complete and accurate picture of the event before jumping
into analysis.
Deep dives can look different depending on the nature of the complaint. The type of issue
determines what information you need, which tools youʼll use, and how youʼll approach the
investigation. But no matter the case, the starting point is always the same: build a clear
Timeline or High-Level Overview of what occurred.
Timeline Creation
Start from the beginning of the event and work forward in chronological order.
Document each key action — what happened, when it happened, and who was involved.
Note the outcome of each action and how it changed the state of the work order.
Highlight gaps or inconsistencies that may point to where the issue originated.
Why this matters: A well-structured timeline makes it easier to pinpoint root cause of
when and where the breakdown occurred — removing guesswork and speeding up
resolution.
Time Tracking: Track the time you spend on each part of the investigation — timeline,
child issues, and summary/conclusion — directly in the Assembly ticket as internal notes.
This ensures we maintain both quality and efficiency across all deep dives.
Donʼt just copy events. Interpret and connect them. Understand why each action was taken
and how it impacted the next step. This extra layer of analysis turns a simple list of actions into a
powerful investigative tool that helps uncover the root cause more quickly and accurately.

Include These Details in Each Timeline Entry
2.
Every entry should contain the critical details needed to clearly understand what happened.
At minimum, capture:
Date and Time: When the action occurred
Action Taken: What happened (e.g., task created, SP sourced, SMS sent, severity
updated)
User: Who performed the action (PM, Associate, SP, Resident, System)
Tool/Link: Direct reference to where the action took place (WO, Slack thread, ticket, etc.)
Evidence: Screenshots, snips, messages, photos, or any relevant details
Keep entries short, structured, and consistent. Focus on actions and results, not opinions
or assumptions.
Create Entries
3.
Once key events are identified, build the timeline in chronological order. Each entry should be
clear enough that another specialist — or any department — could follow the flow without
additional explanation.
For SDDI tickets, skip the full timeline.
Instead, provide a super high-level summary of what happened, focused only on the
specific complaint.
3–5 bullet points are sufficient.
Keep it short, objective, and tied to the residentʼs or PMʼs feedback.
Tips for Creating an Exceptional Timeline
Be Clear & Concise: Use short, factual statements.
Stay Objective: Avoid assumptions or blame in the timeline itself.
Attach Evidence: Support entries with snips, links, or files where needed.
Maintain Chronology: List events in the exact order they occurred.
Make It Skimmable: Another specialist should be able to read and understand it in minutes.

## (3) Identify Gaps & Create Feedback
As part of our primary objective, itʼs critical to diligently identify, document, and communicate
any gaps, errors, or improvement opportunities uncovered during the deep dive. This includes
both core issues tied to the complaint and secondary oversights that may contribute to future
failures.
By capturing and escalating these findings, we can:
Prevent similar issues from recurring
Strengthen internal processes and accountability
Proactively reduce future complaints
This is where Child Issue Tickets are created to document feedback and route it to the right
team for resolution or improvement.
Documenting Issues: Child Issue Tickets vs. Reported Issue Found Tab
Reported Issue Found Note:
Use this when the issue involves external stakeholders or account setup, not internal teams.
Common examples include:
Property Manager or resident errors/misunderstandings
LCN / Preferred Vendor issues
Child Issue Tickets:
Use this when the gap involves Latchel teams, systems, or processes, such as:
Associates or Support
Latchel Vendor Network (LVN)
Product/Ops or platform behavior
Account setup or configuration issues
General process improvement opportunities
Child issue tickets are used to formally document and escalate inappropriate actions,
missed steps, system behavior, or gaps in processes directly tied to the complaint. This
ensures the right team is looped in to:
Correct account settings or configuration,
Address team or vendor performance issues, and
Review and implement potential process improvements.

## (4) Conclude
The Conclusion is the final stage of the deep dive process. It serves as the narrative the
requester will receive — clearly explaining the findings, decisions, and preventive actions
resulting from the investigation.
A strong conclusion provides transparency, demonstrates accountability, and builds trust by
ensuring the requester fully understands what happened, why it happened, and what is being
done to prevent it from happening again.
Key Components of a Strong Conclusion
What Happened?
1.
Provide a clear and concise summary of the original complaint and key investigation details.
Revisit the primary issue raised to ensure the conclusion aligns with the original scope.
Highlight the timeline of critical events and relevant findings uncovered during the
investigation.
Why Did It Happen?
2.
Identify the root cause and contributing factors.
Explain what led to the issue in straightforward language, focusing on facts rather than
assumptions.
Reference supporting evidence (e.g., timelines, communication logs, process gaps)
identified in previous steps.
What Happens Next? (Resolution & Prevention)
3.
Outline the resolution provided to the requester and how their concern is being addressed.
Detail the preventive actions or process improvements implemented to reduce the risk of
recurrence.
Emphasize accountability and reinforce that concrete steps have been taken.

## (5) Close the Deep Dive Ticket
The final step in the deep dive process is closing the ticket properly. This ensures the
investigation is fully documented, the requesting party is notified, and key data is captured for
tracking and reporting. A clean, consistent closeout reflects the professionalism of our team and
ensures nothing falls through the cracks.
With an Assembly Ticket
Post the Conclusion:
1.
Add the conclusion as an internal comment on the Assembly case thread.
Ensure it includes all relevant details and links for easy reference.
Tag the Requester:
2.
Tag the Case Owner or the team member who originally submitted the deep dive request
to ensure visibility.
Update Case Status:
3.
Change the Case Status to “Escalated” to signal that the investigation is complete and
documented.
Include the Following in the Internal Comment:
4.
Conclusion (final narrative summarizing the findings and resolution)
Assembly Deep Dive Link (URL)
Work Order (WO) Link (URL)
Stop the Timer:
5.
Close the Deep Dive Assembly ticket.
Click the Timer button to stop time tracking and log your total handling time.

If the deep dive was submitted outside of Assembly (e.g., via Slack):
