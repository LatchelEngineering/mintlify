# **Overview**

Every DD Conclusion email we send is the result of significant internal effort — timeline research, system audits, vendor coordination. But the way we've been packaging it for customers is driving post-DD complaints. The pattern: we open with "we completed our thorough review," walk through the full chronology, then bury the resolution at the end.

The customer doesn't need a case file. They need to feel heard, understand what went wrong, and know what's happening next.

<Danger>
  **Core issue:** The DD investigation is our internal process. The customer email is their experience. We've been sending them the former when they need the latter.
</Danger>

---

# **What to Stop Doing**

Three habits that are driving follow-up complaints:

1. **Opening with the investigation** — "After completing our thorough review of Work Order #12345..." frames the email around your process, not their problem. Customers experience this as deflection.
2. **Timeline dumps** — A chronological play-by-play of every system event, approval, and vendor action. The customer lived through this. They don't need a recap — they need resolution.
3. **Vague accountability** — "The Payments Team is currently reviewing" or "We apologize for any confusion" without a concrete outcome or owner. This is the #1 driver of follow-up complaints.

---

# **The ACE Framework**

Every DD Conclusion customer email follows three parts, in this order. The investigation findings are your _input_ — not your output.

## **A — Acknowledge the Impact _(not the case)_**

Lead with what happened to the customer, not what you investigated. One to two sentences that name their specific frustration. No pleasantries, no case numbers in the opener.

<Card title="❌ Avoid:" type="danger">
  "We have completed our thorough review of Work Order #15604-1 and want to confirm what happened."
</Card>

<Card title="✅ Instead: " type="check">
  "I understand your team approved an estimate on this work order that you didn't intend to."
</Card>

## **C — Clarify the Cause _(plain English, one paragraph max)_**

What went wrong, stated simply. Own it where Latchel is at fault. Skip the system walk-through and jargon. If a timeline is truly needed, use brief bullet points with timestamps — never prose narration.

<Card title='❌ Avoid: "' type="danger">
  On 4/29, PM Rhea approved Estimate #21466 for \$1,364.61, which covered the collars, dampers, and zone board. That work was completed on 5/4 (Invoice #41938). On the same visit, the vendor reported that the system was still freezing and that they needed to return. On 5/8, the vendor submitted a new estimate of \$1,312.19..."
</Card>

<Card title="✅ Instead: " type="check">
  "Our system auto-approved a second estimate because it fell under your pre-approved budget threshold. It treated the new scope as part of the original job instead of requiring a separate approval. That's a system gap we're actively working to fix."
</Card>

## **E — Execute the Resolution _(concrete action \+ next step)_**

State exactly what we're doing — not what's "being reviewed." Name the resolution, who owns it, and when to expect it. Add a one-sentence prevention note if applicable. This is the part the customer reads most carefully.

<Card title="❌ Avoid: " type="danger">
  "We apologize for any confusion this may have caused. Our Payments Team is currently reviewing the matter and will keep you informed of any updates."
</Card>

<Card title="✅ Instead: " type="check">
  "We're covering the full \$1,312.19 as a one-time exception. You'll see that reflected within \[X days\]. Going forward, cases with a second scope on a work order will need to be split into a new work order — we'll walk your team through that process."
</Card>

# **Pre-Send Checklist**

- Before sending any DD Conclusion email, confirm:
- Does the opening sentence name what the customer experienced — not what you investigated?
- Did you avoid opening with "we reviewed," "after our investigation," or a WO/case number?
- Is the root cause explained in plain English, in 3 sentences or fewer?
- If there's a timeline, is it in bullet format with timestamps — not narrative prose?
- Is there a concrete resolution stated (not "under review" or "being assessed")?
- Is there a named owner and a specific timeframe?
- Does the email end with a statement, not an open question asking the PM what to do next?

# **A Note on the DD Investigation**

None of this changes how thoroughly we investigate. The depth of the DD work is what makes the customer email possible — it gives us the root cause, the timeline, and the facts we need to make confident decisions.

The shift is in how we translate that work for the customer. The investigation findings belong in internal documentation (Jira, ASM notes, the Slack thread). The customer email is a distillation: acknowledge their experience, explain what happened simply, and close with a clear resolution.

<Note>
  **Principle to remember:** Effort is not communication. A detailed investigation summary that leaves the customer without a clear answer is a communication failure, regardless of the work that went into it.
</Note>
