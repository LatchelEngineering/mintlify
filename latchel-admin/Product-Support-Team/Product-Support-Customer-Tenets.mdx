### **Process Overview**

“A _tenet_ is a principle or belief honored by a person or, more often, a group of people.”  It provides generic guidelines for decision making and expectations on how to behave, so the whole team can provide a consistent experience to our customers.

At Latchel, our Customer Service Tenets are summarized into 5 tenets:

1. Treat Every Customer as a Friend
2. Reduce Customer Effort
3. Answer the Question Asked
4. Solve the Problem
5. Escalate Problems

These are the **<u>5 things you need to keep in mind EVERY TIME you are interacting with the customer</u>**! So now, High 5 your customers today:

### **Tenet**1 - Treat Every Customer as a Friend

**_We treat our customers with the same attention and kindness as we treat our friends_**

In the [CEO letter about Customer Obsession](https://latchel.atlassian.net/wiki/spaces/LTG/pages/1119880096/A+Letter+from+the+CEO+Customer+Obsession), Ethan mentions that _“Latchel will become the most Caring, Convenient, Transparent, and Affordable maintenance coordination company to ever exist_.”

This customer service tenet is very related to “Caring” - _“We ALWAYS approach a customer with empathy and respect” - Ethan_

### **Tenet 2 - Reduce Customer Effort**

**_We ensure that the customer experience is easy, simple and time effective_**

In the [CEO letter about Customer Obsession](https://latchel.atlassian.net/wiki/spaces/LTG/pages/1119880096/A+Letter+from+the+CEO+Customer+Obsession), Ethan mentions that _“Latchel will become the most Caring, Convenient, Transparent, and Affordable maintenance coordination company to ever exist_.”

This customer service tenet is very related to “Convenient” and “Transparent”:

- **Convenient**→ Make it easy for customers to interact with us and use our tools. Save customers’ time and energy by simplifying steps and explanations.
- **Transparent**→ Communicate often with honesty and clarity. Keep customers informed about what is happening and why. Build trust by providing clear updates and guidance.

| **Reduce Customer Effort** | **Why is this helpful?** | **Tips & Examples** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Provide Clarity - Be Concise, Direct & Specific** | Ensuring that information is **crystal clear and concise** is crucial for effective communication. **Clarity is often mistakenly equated with accuracy**, but while accuracy ensures the truthfulness of the information, **clarity ensures its understandability**. An accurate response may not be helpful if the recipient cannot comprehend or apply it. Therefore, **clarity is as important as accuracy**. By delivering **concise, direct, and fact-based messages**, you minimize misunderstandings and reduce the need for follow-up clarifying questions. This approach not only saves time but also helps the customer understand the actions being taken to address their concerns, **fostering confidence in your abilities and building trust**. | - **Listen, Paraphrase, and Prioritize**: Start by actively listening to the customer and paraphrasing their requests to confirm understanding. Address multiple requests one at a time, saying, “Let’s start with \[XYZ\].” - **Focus on Solutions and Simplicity**: Emphasize what you can do rather than what you cannot. Offer alternative solutions when necessary, but limit options to 2 or 3 to avoid overwhelming the customer. Keep explanations simple and avoid unnecessary details unless requested. - **Be Specific and Fact-Based**: Provide clear, factual information by referencing specific details such as property addresses, dates, and involved parties. Ensure all information is accurate and supported by documentation. - **Communicate Clearly and Concisely**: Before sending written communication, review it for clarity and conciseness. Rearrange, modify, and remove unnecessary text, and check for grammar and typos to ensure the message is easy to understand. - **Use Plain Language**: Use plain, everyday language instead of internal jargon to ensure clear communication with all parties involved. Do **not** use abbreviations:   - Write out “Emergency Escalation Contacts” instead of “EEC”). |
| **Be Transparent** | **Transparency builds trust** by showing the customer that action is being taken behind the scenes. When we **proactively share updates**, it reassures the customer that their issue is being worked on — **without them needing to ask**. This reduces their effort and prevents frustration. **Clear communication** about what’s happening, **why it’s taking time**, and **what they can expect next** turns wait time into something that feels **legitimate and supported**, rather than ignored. | - Don’t make customers ask, _“What’s going on?”_ — update them first. - Send timely updates that explain:   - What you’re doing   - Why it’s taking time   - What to expect next Example: _“We’re still waiting on vendor confirmation. ### **Tenet 3 - Answer the Question Asked**

We carefully evaluate each customer’s question so we can ensure we’re answering **the right question** — not just what we assume they meant.

Sometimes, it’s easy to jump into action based on what we think the customer is asking. But when we **slow down and truly understand their concern**, we can provide more accurate, helpful, and complete answers. This avoids unnecessary back-and-forth, builds trust, and shows the customer they are being heard.

| **Answer the Question Asked** | **Why is this helpful?** | **Tips & Examples** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Accurate & Knowledgable** | The information we provide must be **true, helpful, and directly applicable** to the customer's situation. While **speed matters**, it should never come at the cost of accuracy. Customers rely on us not just for fast answers — but for the **right** answers. When we’re knowledgeable and careful in our responses, we build confidence, prevent errors, and reduce the need for follow-up. | - \*\*Don’t sacrifice accuracy for speed:\*\*It’s better to take a little extra time to provide a correct and thorough answer than to rush and risk misinformation. - \*\*Know the product inside and out:\*\*The more you understand Latchel’s services, tools, and policies, the better equipped you'll be to answer questions without hesitation — and without escalating unnecessarily. - \*\*Keep learning:\*\*If you're unsure of something, don’t guess. Look it up, ask, or escalate. Each case is a chance to build your knowledge and serve the next customer even better. - \*\*Be precise and confident:\*\*Avoid vague or generic responses. Tailor your answer to the customer’s specific situation and back it up with clear reasoning or reference materials when appropriate. |
| **Thorough Investigation** | A **thorough investigation** starts with carefully reading the customer’s message to fully understand what they’re asking for — even if they don’t spell it out clearly. When you slow down and read with intention, you’ll be able to identify every **question**, **request**, and **concern** the customer has. Customers may write long, emotional, or indirect messages. Your role is to **analyze the message**, **distill the key actions**, and determine what needs to be done. This approach allows you to respond with precision and prevents unnecessary back-and-forth, delays, or misunderstandings. | - \*\*Read thoroughly before responding:\*\*Don’t skim. Read the full message, then go back and highlight or list the specific action items the customer is asking for. - \*\*Take notes while reading:\*\*Note any requests, concerns, or keywords (e.g., “add,” “remove,” “I need,” “can you help,” etc.) to ensure nothing is missed. - \*\*Read between the lines:\*\*Sometimes customers don’t clearly say what they need — they may tell a story, express frustration, or jump between topics. It’s up to you to translate that into actionable items. - \*\*Paraphrase to confirm understanding:\*\*If something is unclear, summarize your understanding of what they want, then ask:   - _“It sounds like you’d like us to remove the vendor from this work order and notify the resident about the update — do I have that right?”_ - \*\*Avoid assumptions:\*\*When in doubt, clarify. It’s better to ask a quick follow-up than to act on the wrong assumption. |
| **Recap the Customers' Question** | Paraphrasing the customer’s questions **confirms your understanding** of what they are asking and expecting from you. This helps avoid wasting time for both you and the customer on unnecessary back-and-forth or misunderstandings. Often, customers’ requests aren’t entirely clear. By paraphrasing, you ensure that **you and the customer are on the same page** before moving forward. | - Use your **own words** to briefly restate what you understand the customer’s request to be. This sounds more natural and avoids robotic repetition. - Avoid acronyms or internal language — use simple language that any customer can easily understand. - Combine an apology with your summary when appropriate (apologies are most effective when genuine; if you don’t feel it, show appreciation for the customer’s patience instead).   - For example:_“I understand how frustrating it is that you can’t remove this resident. I will take care of that for you. To confirm, is the resident John Smith at 123 Main St?”_ - Use **affirmative sentences followed by yes/no confirmation questions** to reduce ambiguity.   - For example: _“Okay, you want me to add the property at \[address\] to your account, correct?”_ - For simple issues, **combine paraphrasing with your next steps**:   - _“I will cancel the clogged toilet portion of your work order since you fixed it, but will keep the leaking kitchen sink portion open. Is that correct?”_ - When multiple requests are involved, **summarize the total number and paraphrase each one separately**. - For example: _“I understand you have two requests. First, \[paraphrase request one\] — is that right? Second, \[paraphrase request two\] — is that correct?”_ - For email, **begin your reply by introducing yourself and paraphrasing** to summarize your understanding and the solution since you won’t get immediate confirmation. |

### Tenet 4 – Solve the Problem

We strive to fulfill our customers’ needs and resolve their concerns effectively.

While we aim to resolve every issue, some problems may be outside our control or subject to approvals beyond our authority.

Our goal is to:

- **Answer all questions and address all concerns**, including any related or account issues that arise.
- **Prevent new problems** from occurring as we resolve the initial issue.
- **Ensure the customer understands and agrees with the resolution.**
- **Help the customer leave the interaction feeling positive or at least that the resolution was fair and reasonable.**

| --- | --- | --- |
| **Seek Help & Use Resources** | When you encounter issues beyond your control or do not know the answer, **don’t hesitate to seek help and escalate** to the appropriate team. | **Confirm the Problem** | If you’re not clear on the exact problem the customer wants resolved, you risk solving the wrong issue—or even creating new problems—while the original concern remains unaddressed. Paraphrase the problem early to confirm understanding. - Ask clear questions if details are unclear. - Avoid assumptions; always verify the customer’s intent. - Combine paraphrasing with next steps to align expectations. |
| **Take Action - Ensure ALL Problems & Questions are Resolved** | The customer reached out with specific issues or requests—ensuring these are fully addressed avoids frustration and repeat contacts. | - Review your notes carefully to ensure every question or issue has been addressed. - When you resolve an issue, confirm the customer’s satisfaction by saying, “I’ve completed \[action\]. Does this resolve your concern? Is there anything else I can help you with today?” |
| **Quality Over Speed** | Respecting our customers’ time by resolving issues promptly builds trust and satisfaction. However, **prioritizing quality over speed ensures the problem is truly fixed and prevents repeated contacts.** Clear communication about next steps and follow-ups keeps customers informed and confident, while seeking help when needed guarantees the best outcome. | - If the issue can’t be resolved right away, clearly explain the next steps to the customer and offer to follow up. For example: “_I need to do some deeper research to resolve this. Would it be okay if I get back to you within X hours/by tomorrow?_” - If you already know what needs to be done, share those next steps with the customer. This builds trust and shows you have a plan. ### **Tenet**5 - Escalate Problems

**_We seek help and escalate potential or actual problems as they happen._**

### **What escalation means**

Escalation is the act of increasing the urgency, visibility, or level of intervention for a problem.When we escalate, the problem is either out of our control or risks getting worse without immediate support.

You should escalate when:

- You’ve done your research and still don’t know what to do,
- You don’t have access or authority to take the next step,
- The customer is demanding to speak with a supervisor or CSM.
- There is an obvious sense of frustration, irritation, and/or anger.

In short, escalate when **you can’t move the issue forward**, or when **you believe there is a churn risk**. These are signs that the problem needs a higher level of attention—fast.

Escalation can take many forms: looping in a team lead, submitting an internal ticket, or flagging a trend you’ve spotted. It’s not always about asking someone else to “take over”—it’s often about **bringing in the right partner to help resolve the issue quickly and correctly.**

| **Show Empathy** | Customers will contact us either when they have an issue they are struggling with or a request that they feel they can’t solve on their own. Sometimes those will be simple things, sometimes those will be more complex or repetitive issues that have deeply frustrated the customer. You won’t know how the customer feels until you \*\*<u>listen</u>\*\*to them and are able to empathize with them.  Being empathetic means that you are able to understand and relate to their feelings, i.e. you can put yourself in their situation and feel what they are feeling. That is what you usually do when you are listening and supporting a friend. When you do that, you are able to choose your words and actions more appropriately, so your friend feels you truly care about them. | **Ask Questions & Take Interest** | Asking questions shows you care about _getting it right._ When you **don’t make assumptions** and instead take the time to ask, you: - Avoid misunderstandings - Show the customer you value accuracy - Ensure you’re solving the _real_ issue — not just what it looks like on the surface It also builds trust. Customers feel heard when we take interest and ask for details instead of brushing them off with a quick fix. Asking questions and taking interest turns a surface-level response into a personalized, meaningful experience. It shows the customer we’re invested in solving _their_ problem — not just closing a ticket. | **Don’t assume — ask!** If you’re unclear about something, asking questions helps avoid mistakes and shows the customer you care about getting it right. ✅ _“Just to make sure I understand correctly, are you saying…”_ ✅ _“Can you clarify what happened right before the issue started?”_ ❌ _“I think this is probably what they meant.”_ ❌ _Taking action without confirming key details_ \_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_ **If we made a mistake — or if the customer\_feels\_we did — own it.** A genuine apology builds trust. ✅ _“I’m really sorry for the confusion — that’s on us.”_ ✅ _“I hear your frustration, and I’m sorry this didn’t go the way you expected.”_ ❌ _“That’s not our fault.”_ ❌ _“There’s nothing we can do — that’s just how it works.”_ \_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_ **Dig deeper to solve or escalate effectively.** After apologizing, ask thoughtful questions to gather the details needed to move forward. ✅ _“Can you walk me through what’s happened so far so I can look into this fully?”_ ✅ _“Thanks for sharing that — I’m going to do a deep dive and follow up shortly.”_ |
| **Positive Position** | Your words shape how the customer feels — not just about the situation, but about _us._ When you focus on what you **can** do rather than what you **can’t**, you: - Keep the conversation solution-oriented - Help the customer feel hopeful and supported - Build confidence that they’re in good hands Each customer deserves to feel like their problem matters — because it does.
