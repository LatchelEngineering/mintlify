# **Step-by-Step Guide**

## **(1) Receive the Submission & Review the Complaint**

When a Deep Dive (DD) request is received, the Product Support Specialist carefully reviews the **specific issue or inquiry** to ensure a clear understanding of:

- The problem being raised
- The impact of the issue
- Any supporting information provided by the customer

**Best Practice:** Always read the **title and description** of the ticket first to get an overview of the deep dive you’re about to conduct.

<Info>
  **Visibility:** Keep the **Team Owner and Viewer** set to `PS Deep Dive Tickets` to ensure the entire Product Support team can view and collaborate as needed.
</Info>

### **When reviewing a Deep Dive ticket, follow these steps:**

1. In the Assembly ticket, go to the **Attributes** section (right panel) and click **START**button on the timer when you begin reviewing.

- Use the same button to **pause** the timer during breaks or other activities.

<Warning>
  **Why it matters:**

  - Timer tracking powers our **Handling Time** metric.
  - Our goal is a working time of within **4 hours** for deep dives.
</Warning>

<img
  src="attachments/3214082067/3642196024.png?width=760"
  alt="image-20250925-183153.png"
  width="760"
/>

2. Assign yourself as the **Owner** of the ticket.
3. Under **Labels**, check the box to categorize as either **DD (Deep Dive)** or **SDDI**.

<img
  src="attachments/3214082067/3642425379.png?width=322"
  alt="image-20250925-183438.png"
  width="322"
/>

4. To select the **Complaint Category**, click the **\+ icon** and search for the appropriate category.

- Refer to the guidelines outlined in the [<u>Job Aid: PS DD and SDDI Category Matrix</u>](2480144415.html).

<img
  src="attachments/3214082067/3642163310.png?width=329"
  alt="image-20250925-183646.png"
  width="329"
/>

5. Keep the ticket in:

- **Needs Response** — Brand new ticket submissions not yet attended to (backlog).
- **Open** — Tickets you are actively working on.

<Warning>
  **Why it matters:**

  - There are SLAs in place for DDs: **3-day SLA**.
  - If a ticket remains in **Needs Response** and approaches the 3-day mark, **Assembly will flag it as Breaching**.
  - This is how we track our **Completion Time** metric.
</Warning>

## **(2) Use Resources & Build the Timeline**

The first step in any deep dive is to **gather all relevant information** surrounding the issue. This means pulling data from every available source — including support tickets, customer communications, system logs, data analytics, and any other resources that can help clarify what happened. The goal is to build a **complete and accurate picture** of the event before jumping into analysis.

Deep dives can look different depending on the nature of the complaint. The type of issue determines **what information you need**, **which tools you’ll use**, and **how you’ll approach the investigation**. But no matter the case, the starting point is always the same: build a clear **Timeline or High-Level Overview** of what occurred.

---

### **Timeline Creation**

- **Start from the beginning** of the event and work forward in chronological order.
- **Document each key action** — what happened, when it happened, and who was involved.
- **Note the outcome** of each action and how it changed the state of the work order.
- **Highlight gaps or inconsistencies** that may point to where the issue originated.

<Warning>
  **Why this matters:** A well-structured timeline makes it easier to pinpoint root cause of when and where the breakdown occurred — removing guesswork and speeding up resolution.
</Warning>

<Note>
  \*\*Time Tracking:\*\*Track the time you spend on each part of the investigation — timeline, child issues, and summary/conclusion — directly in the Assembly ticket as **internal notes**. This ensures we maintain both **quality** and **efficiency** across all deep dives.
</Note>

Don’t just copy events. **Interpret and connect** them. Understand _why_ each action was taken and how it impacted the next step. This extra layer of analysis turns a simple list of actions into a **powerful investigative tool** that helps uncover the root cause more quickly and accurately.

---

### Building the Timeline:

1. **Gather Information**

Start by collecting all available data sources related to the issue. Pull everything you need to fully understand the sequence of events:

1. Related **work orders** and Assembly tickets
2. **Customer communications** (emails, SMS, calls, webchat transcripts)
3. **Internal threads** (Slack posts, escalation notes)
4. **System activity logs** (settings updates, task creation, dispatch events, action sets, automation triggers)
5. Any **attachments or evidence** (photos, estimates, invoices, vendor notes, access instrcutions)

<Info>
  **Pro Tip:** You can use [ChatGPT](https://chat.openai.com/) to help structure and sequence the data faster — but **always** **verify against the original sources** before finalizing the timeline.
</Info>

---

2. **Include These Details in Each Timeline Entry**

- Every entry should contain the critical details needed to clearly understand what happened. At minimum, capture:
  - **Date and Time:** When the action occurred
  - **Action Taken:** What happened (e.g., task created, SP sourced, SMS sent, severity updated)
  - **User:** Who performed the action (PM, Associate, SP, Resident, System)
  - **Tool/Link:** Direct reference to where the action took place (WO, Slack thread, ticket, etc.)
  - **Evidence:** Screenshots, snips, messages, photos, or any relevant details

<Note>
  Keep entries short, structured, and consistent. Focus on actions and results, not opinions or assumptions.
</Note>

---

3. **Create Entries**

Once key events are identified, **build the timeline in chronological order**. Each entry should be clear enough that another specialist — or any department — could follow the flow without additional explanation.

---

<Tip>
  For **SDDI tickets**, **skip the full timeline.** Instead, provide a **super high-level summary** of what happened, focused only on the specific complaint.

  - 3–5 bullet points are sufficient.
  - Keep it short, objective, and tied to the resident’s or PM’s feedback.
</Tip>

### Tips for Creating an Exceptional Timeline

- **Be Clear & Concise:** Use short, factual statements.
- **Stay Objective:** Avoid assumptions or blame in the timeline itself.
- **Attach Evidence:** Support entries with snips, links, or files where needed.
- **Maintain Chronology:** List events in the exact order they occurred.
- **Make It Skimmable:** Another specialist should be able to read and understand it in minutes.

---

## **(3) Identify Gaps & Create Feedback**

As part of our **primary objective**, it’s critical to diligently identify, document, and communicate **any gaps, errors, or improvement opportunities** uncovered during the deep dive. This includes both **core issues tied to the complaint** and **secondary oversights** that may contribute to future failures.

By capturing and escalating these findings, we can:

- Prevent similar issues from recurring
- Strengthen internal processes and accountability
- Proactively reduce future complaints

This is where **Child Issue Tickets** are created to document feedback and route it to the right team for resolution or improvement.

---

### **Documenting Issues: Child Issue Tickets vs. Reported Issue Found Tab**

- **Reported Issue Found Note**: Use this when the issue involves **external stakeholders or account setup**, not internal teams. Common examples include:
  - Property Manager or resident errors/misunderstandings
  - LCN / Preferred Vendor issues
- **Child Issue Tickets**: Use this when the gap involves **Latchel teams, systems, or processes**, such as: Child issue tickets are used to **formally document and escalate** inappropriate actions, missed steps, system behavior, or gaps in processes **directly tied to the complaint**. This ensures the right team is looped in to:
  - Associates or Support
  - Latchel Vendor Network (LVN)
  - Product/Ops or platform behavior
  - Account setup or configuration issues
  - General process improvement opportunities
  - Correct account settings or configuration,
  - Address team or vendor performance issues, and
  - Review and implement potential process improvements.

---

## **(4) Conclude**

The **Conclusion** is the final stage of the deep dive process. It serves as the **narrative the requester will receive** — clearly explaining the findings, decisions, and preventive actions resulting from the investigation.

A strong conclusion provides transparency, demonstrates accountability, and builds trust by ensuring the requester fully understands what happened, why it happened, and what is being done to prevent it from happening again.

### **Key Components of a Strong Conclusion**

1. **What Happened?**

- Provide a clear and concise summary of the original complaint and key investigation details.
- Revisit the primary issue raised to ensure the conclusion aligns with the original scope.
- Highlight the timeline of critical events and relevant findings uncovered during the investigation.

2. **Why Did It Happen?**

- Identify the **root cause** and contributing factors.
- Explain what led to the issue in straightforward language, focusing on facts rather than assumptions.
- Reference supporting evidence (e.g., timelines, communication logs, process gaps) identified in previous steps.

3. **What Happens Next? (Resolution & Prevention)**

- Outline the **resolution** provided to the requester and how their concern is being addressed.
- Detail the **preventive actions** or process improvements implemented to reduce the risk of recurrence.
- Emphasize accountability and reinforce that concrete steps have been taken.

</Note>

## **(5) Close the Deep Dive Ticket**

The final step in the deep dive process is **closing the ticket properly**. This ensures the investigation is fully documented, the requesting party is notified, and key data is captured for tracking and reporting. A clean, consistent closeout reflects the professionalism of our team and ensures nothing falls through the cracks.

#### **With an Assembly Ticket**

1. **Post the Conclusion:**

- Add the conclusion as an **internal comment** on the Assembly case thread.
- Ensure it includes all relevant details and links for easy reference.

2. **Tag the Requester:**

- Tag the **Case Owner** or the team member who originally submitted the deep dive request to ensure visibility.

3. **Update Case Status:**

- Change the Case Status to **“Escalated”** to signal that the investigation is complete and documented.

4. **Include the Following in the Internal Comment:**

- **Conclusion** (final narrative summarizing the findings and resolution)
- **Assembly Deep Dive Link** (URL)
- **Work Order (WO) Link** (URL)

5. **Stop the Timer:**

- Close the Deep Dive Assembly ticket.
- Click the **Timer** button to stop time tracking and log your total handling time.

#### **Without an Assembly Ticket**

If the deep dive was submitted outside of Assembly (e.g., via Slack):

1. **Post the Conclusion** directly in the **ASM thread** where the request originated - It will post as a response in the Slack workflow submission.
2. Include all **relevant links** (e.g., Work Order, related case URLs) and a **clear, structured summary**.
3. Tag the requester to notify them that the deep dive has been completed.
4. Update any relevant internal trackers or documentation if required.
